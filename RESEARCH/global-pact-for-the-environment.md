# Global Pact for the Environment

## Overview

The Global Pact for the Environment is a proposed international legal framework intended to consolidate and strengthen fundamental principles of environmental law.

Unlike the Earth Charter, which focuses primarily on ethical values, the Global Pact seeks to establish legal rights, duties, and responsibilities that can guide governments, institutions, organizations, and individuals.

Its central idea is that a healthy environment should not only be an aspiration but a legal right.

---

## Core Vision

The Global Pact recognizes that environmental protection is inseparable from human well-being.

Rather than treating environmental harm as a secondary concern, it frames environmental protection as a matter of rights, duties, and justice.

The document attempts to create a common legal foundation that could guide national and international law.

---

## Key Principles

### 1. Right to an Ecologically Sound Environment

Every person has the right to live in an environment adequate for health, dignity, well-being, and development.

Environmental degradation is therefore not only an ecological issue but also a human rights issue.

---

### 2. Duty to Take Care of the Environment

Rights are accompanied by responsibilities.

Individuals, institutions, corporations, and governments all share a duty to protect and improve the environment.

---

### 3. Principle of Prevention

Environmental harm should be prevented before it occurs.

Preventing damage is preferable to repairing damage after the fact.

---

### 4. Precautionary Principle

When there is a risk of serious or irreversible harm, lack of complete scientific certainty should not be used as a reason to delay protective action.

This principle recognizes that waiting for perfect evidence may sometimes lead to irreversible consequences.

---

### 5. Polluter Pays Principle

Those who cause environmental harm should bear the costs of preventing, mitigating, and repairing that harm.

The burden should not be transferred to society or future generations.

---

### 6. Access to Environmental Information

People should have access to information about environmental conditions, risks, and decisions that affect them.

Transparency is considered essential for accountability.

---

### 7. Public Participation

Individuals and communities should have opportunities to participate in environmental decision-making processes.

Environmental governance should not be limited to governments alone.

---

## Key Contributions

### Rights and Responsibilities

The Global Pact explicitly links environmental rights with environmental duties.

### Prevention over Repair

It shifts focus from responding to damage toward avoiding damage.

### Accountability

It introduces mechanisms that can support legal responsibility for environmental harm.

### Legal Framework

Unlike many ethical declarations, the Pact attempts to translate principles into legal language.

---

## Strengths

* Strong legal orientation.
* Clear articulation of rights and duties.
* Emphasis on prevention rather than remediation.
* Recognition of accountability.
* Applicable across national legal systems.

---

## Limitations

* Focuses primarily on environmental issues.
* Provides principles but limited enforcement mechanisms.
* Does not fully address technological, social, cultural, or geopolitical forms of long-term harm.
* Future generations are indirectly considered but not central.

---

## Lessons for a Constitution of a Living World

The Global Pact demonstrates that responsibility can be expressed in legal terms rather than solely ethical ones.

Its greatest contribution is the recognition that rights alone are insufficient; duties and accountability are also necessary.

However, the Pact remains largely focused on environmental impacts.

A future Constitution of a Living World may extend similar principles to all forms of long-term consequences, including technological, economic, social, and civilizational impacts.

---

## Potential Contributions to a Constitution of a Living World

### Principle of Responsibility

Freedom must be accompanied by responsibility for foreseeable consequences.

### Principle of Prevention

Avoiding harm is preferable to compensating for harm.

### Principle of Accountability

Those who create harm should participate in its repair.

### Principle of Transparency

People have a right to understand decisions that affect the conditions of life.
